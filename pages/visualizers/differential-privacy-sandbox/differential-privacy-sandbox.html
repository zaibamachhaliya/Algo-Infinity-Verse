Differential Privacy Sandbox

How do you publish statistics without revealing anyone's secret?

You lie. Just enough. Differential Privacy adds carefully calibrated noise to every query result so that no individual's data can be reverse-engineered from the output — while the aggregate statistic remains useful. It's the mechanism behind Apple's telemetry, Google's RAPPOR, and the US Census. Watch it work, break it, and tune it.

Attacker Panel — "Remove One Person" Attack

Without DP: an attacker queries the database with and without a specific person. If the results differ, they learn that person's value exactly. With DP: the noisy answers are so close that the attacker can't reliably distinguish whether Alice is in the dataset or not.

🔐 The Math Behind Differential Privacy

📐

The Definition

A mechanism M satisfies ε-differential privacy if for any two datasets D and D' differing by one person, and any output S: P[M(D)∈S] ≤ eᵉ × P[M(D')∈S]. Small ε = outputs barely differ regardless of any individual. The privacy guarantee is mathematical, not a policy promise.

🔔

The Laplace Mechanism

For a numeric query with sensitivity Δf (max change one person can cause), add noise drawn from Laplace(0, Δf/ε). Sensitivity of mean of n values = max_value/n. Sensitivity of count = 1. Lower ε → wider Laplace → more noise → more privacy.

🏦

Real Deployments

Apple uses DP with ε≈1-4 for keyboard and emoji telemetry. The US Census 2020 used DP instead of suppression. Google's RAPPOR uses randomized response (local DP) for Chrome statistics. Each query into these systems costs some of a finite privacy budget.

⚠️

Budget Composition

The fundamental limit: if you answer k queries each with privacy cost ε, the total cost is at most kε (sequential composition theorem). Averaging k noisy answers removes noise at rate 1/√k — so unlimited repeated queries eventually reveal the truth.
